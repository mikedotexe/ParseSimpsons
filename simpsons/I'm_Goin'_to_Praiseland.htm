"I'm Goin' to Praiseland" is the nineteenth episode of the twelfth season of The Simpsons. It originally premiered on May 6, 2001 on the Fox Broadcasting Company.

In the episode, Ned Flanders builds a Christian-themed amusement park to commemorate his dead wife, Maude Flanders.

"I'm Goin' to Praiseland" is the last episode written by Julie Thacker (the wife of showrunner Mike Scully) and directed by Chuck Sheetz. It is also the second guest appearance of Shawn Colvin as Rachel Jordan, who first appeared in "Alone Again, Natura-Diddily" from season 11. The episode received negative reviews from critics.

[edit] Plot

During an ice cream social at the church, Ned Flanders is reunited with Rachel Jordan. She stays with Flanders instead of going to a motel (at the insistence of The Simpsons), but Rachel leaves after being creeped out over Ned's obsession with making Rachel look like his deceased wife, Maude (including making Rachel sleep on the side of the bed where Maude was and cutting Rachel's hair in Maude's style). Flanders asks The Simpsons to help him forget about Maude by throwing away everything in the house that reminds him of her. Homer, always wanting things done fast instead of properly, uses a woodchipper to get rid of Maude's Earthly possessions (and crudely alters Ned's wedding picture of Maude by taping a picture of himself where Maude's head is), except for a sketchbook. Flanders looks through his late wife's many sketches until he finds designs for a Christian theme park, Praiseland. At the insistence of The Simpsons (again), Flanders decides to realize Maude's dream by building a Christian theme park in her honor.

Flanders builds the park where Storytime Village (which has not been seen or mentioned on the show since season seven's "Lisa the Vegetarian" and is now an abandoned wreck) used to be and asks people from all over town to contribute anything that can be useful for the park. Various citizens pitch in and donate.

When Praiseland is finally opened, the townspeople are put off by its wholesomeness and leave — until everyone sees a Maude mask rise up in front of a memorial statue of Maude (which Flanders uses to raise money for the orphanage). Eventually, anyone who stands in front of the statue collapses, writhes and has his or her own personal vision of Heaven (after speaking in tongues): Principal Skinner tells of being in a perfect elementary school where no one is late, the teacher's lounge is spacious, and Bart is not there to bother him; Disco Stu dances with Frank Sinatra at a discothèque version of Heaven [which is actually Hell for Sinatra]; and The Comic Book Guy saves Captain Kirk from his malfunctioning chair aboard the Starship Enterprise and is offered sex with Uhura from the original Star Trek series, Catwoman from the 1960s Batman TV show, and Agent 99 from Get Smart.

Flanders explains to his children that this is God's will... until he notices Homer trying to work the gas grill at a concession stand. He then discovers that the gas line (near Maude's statue) is leaking gas, causing everyone to inhale the fumes. Flanders tries to shut the place down, but Homer points out that Praiseland has brought everyone together, regardless of race, creed, color, and socioeconomic status. However, the joy is short-lived when Homer spots the orphans (Patches and Poor Violet from "Miracle on Evergreen Terrace") lighting a candle near the leaking gas line and tackle them before an explosion can happen. The townspeople believe Ned and Homer have maliciously assaulted the children and the park suffers.

Ned finally shuts down Praiseland, and Rachel (who is now wearing a wig to cover up the Maude-like haircut from earlier in the episode) returns to ask Flanders on a date. Flanders accepts, after the two of them get rid of Maude's imprint on the bed by tightening the sheets.

[edit] Production

The park is based on Heritage USA.

The episode is written by Julie Thacker, wife of then show runner Mike Scully and directed by Chuck Sheetz. The episode and "Praiseland" were inspired by the Christian park, Heritage USA.[1] The bible gum product was real unbeknown to the writers and were sued by the owners of the company.[1] The gas leak in Praiseland is base on a real life event in Disney World.[2]

Originally there was an alternate ending to "I'm Goin' to Praiseland" with a killer named the Gentleman's Killer being killed by the gas and Wiggum celebrating the death of the celebrity by smoking a cigar above the gas leak and "Praiseland" explodes although no one is injured except for Ned Flanders. is see on the season 12 dvd [3] Ian Maxtone-Graham is seen in the line for heaven.[4]

[edit] Cultural references

The title of the episode is a spoof of the "I'm going to Disneyland" advertising slogan used by The Walt Disney Company. The whole concept of Praiseland is based on the Christian theme park, Heritage USA, that was built in the 1970s by donations made to televangelists Jim and Tammy Faye Bakker. The title of the episode is a spoof of the Paul Simon song Graceland, in which the lyrics "I'm goin' to Graceland" are present. The scene where Ned cuts Rachel's hair to model Maude's hair refers to Vertigo, where an insane man models his new love after his deceased lover. The end sequence song (and the song that plays when Disco Stu hallucinates that he is being let into a disco version of Heaven) is "Get Dancin'" by Disco-Tex and the Sex-O-Lettes. The song also appeared in Bart vs. Thanksgiving from season two.

[edit] Reception

The episode has received negative reviews from critics. Nancy Basile said the episode was Not Must See TV and said she loathed it.[6]
