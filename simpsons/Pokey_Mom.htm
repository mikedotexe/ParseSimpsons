"Pokey Mom" is the tenth episode of The Simpsons' twelfth season, aired on January 14, 2001. In the episode, Marge tries to rehabilitate a convict with remarkable artistic talent. Meanwhile, Homer uses a trash can to fix people's spines.

The episode was written by Tom Martin and directed by Bob Anderson. It also features Michael Keaton as Jack Crowley, Charles Napier as The Warden, Bruce Vilanch as himself, Robert Schimmel as Prisoner. "Pokey Mom" has received mixed to negative reviews from critics.

[edit] Plot

The family attends a prison rodeo. Marge meets Jack Crowley, a convict whom she believes to have great artistic potential after becoming impressed with his work. Marge later teaches a class on being an artist to the prisoners. Jack is up for parole and, with Marge's help, is granted parole under her custody. When Jack is looking for a job, Marge finds a mural-painting job at Springfield Elementary School. Jack paints a powerful, epic mural symbolizing school spirit with a warrior woman riding a puma, which the whole school likes (including the teachers), but Principal Skinner (who hates it) demands that he tone it down using Skinner's own childish idea of kittens in a fantasy land. Jack reluctantly gives into Skinner's wishes; however, upon its unveiling, the new mural is panned by everyone in town (even Ned Flanders considers it too "sugary"). Skinner (because he's Principal Skinner) blames Jack and fires him.

Sometime later, the mural is set on fire by a mystery arsonist; everyone immediately assumes that Jack did it to get back at Skinner. While the police are out searching for Jack, Marge stumbles upon him hiding in the playground. He swears to her that he did not start the fire, and Marge decides to believe him. She then offers to distract Skinner and Chief Wiggum while he escapes; however, instead, he immediately sets fire to Skinner's car, and dances around it laughing maniacally. Jack is arrested, and Marge is furious that he lied to her. When Jack attempts to lie to her again−claiming this time that although he did burn the mural, he did not burn Skinner's car (despite the fact that she had just seen him do it)−she washes her hands of him forever, and Chief Wiggum drives him back to jail.

Meanwhile, Homer suffers from a back injury at the rodeo, and he sees a chiropractor. He reveals that he can solve his back problems with the garbage can; therefore, he names it "Dr. Homer's Miracle Spine-o-Cylinder", and makes a business out of it. It turns out be successful until the other chiropractors denounce it and destroy it.

[edit] Production

"Pokey Mom" was written by Tom Martin and directed by Bob Anderson. The man who wants to smell Marge's clothes is Robert Schimmel.[1] The episode featured various complaints from chiropractors, the biggest amount of complaints since season 10's "Sunday, Cruddy Sunday" and its racy commercial for The Catholic Church.[2] The episode's sub-plot was based on Tim's trip to the chiropractor and during the original script Homer fell on a table instead of a trash can for his get-rich-quick scheme.[3] In the original script, Homer was supposed to get a job as a prison rodeo clown.[3] After the episode aired Tim Long gave Tom Martin an apron book.[3] The last scene of Jack Crowley and Chief Wiggum discussing cable TV shows was completely improvised.[4]

[edit] Deleted scene

  • Marge hopes the prison rodeo is as good as the Renaissance Fair at the loony bin and a prison man offers the family a shift.[5]
  • Homer questions if the chiropractor is a real doctor saying they are not called by their real names and that they do not steal people's wallets.[5]
  • Snake is leaving the jail and asks for his gun; the Sarge asks if he had eaten more cookies or released more criminals.[5]

[edit] Reception

This episode was criticized in the Canadian Chiropractor newsletter for promoting "amateur joint adjustment" and negative stereotypes of chiropractors.[6] Colin Jacobson of DVD Movie Guide gave the episode a mixed review, writing "Other than a nice guest turn from Michael Keaton, “Mom” doesn’t do much to stand out from the crowd. Oh, like much of Season 12, it keeps us interested, but that’s not exactly a strong endorsement. “Mom” provides a watchable show but nothing more."[7] Judge Mac McEntire said the best moment of the episode was the quote "The shapely female form has no place in art!"[8]
