"Bart to the Future" is the seventeenth episode of the 11th season of The Simpsons. The title of the episode is a reference to Back to the Future. It is the second of three future-themed Simpsons episodes (along with "Lisa's Wedding" and "Future-Drama").

This episode aired five years to the day after season six's “Lisa's Wedding”, which also showed The Simpsons' (and Springfield's) life in the distant future. Unlike "Lisa's Wedding" (which was hailed by critics as a good episode), "Bart to the Future" was dubbed one of the worst Simpsons episodes ever by Entertainment Weekly.[1]

[edit] Plot

The Simpsons are going to Larval Lake on a picnic. When they arrive, however, they find that mosquitoes have overrun the park (even stealing a park ranger's class ring and stripping the flesh and muscle from one of his hands). While driving back, the Simpsons find an Indian casino. Homer and Bart go in (leaving Marge in the car because of her gambling addiction, and Lisa in the car because of her age and her conflict over the ethics behind Indian gambling), but Bart is turned away because of his age. Bart then finds Arthur Crandall (the ventriloquist from "Krusty Gets Kancelled") and sneaks in through Gabbo the dummy's case. During a performance, Bart bursts out of the dummy case and gets caught by casino guards. Bart is sent to the casino manager's office, where the manager (who knows Bart's name after Homer listed Bart as collateral while putting down a second mortgage) shows Bart a vision of his future if Bart does not change his ways.

Thirty years into the future, Bart is a 40-year-old beer-drinking slacker trying to launch his music career after dropping out of the DeVry Institute and has resorted to mooching off his parents and Ned Flanders (who is now blind from botched laser eye surgery and is aware that his sons, Rod and Todd Flanders, are gay, but believes he is keeping it a secret between himself and Bart) for money. The only gig Bart can get is at a beach bar owned by Nelson Muntz, and even then, Bart is only paid in popcorn shrimp. Bart lives with Ralph Wiggum (who now has brown hair and does not speak in as many moronic non-sequiturs as he did when he was younger) in a beach cottage by the shore, from which Bart finds out that he has been evicted after his disastrous concert at Nelson's beach bar.

Meanwhile, 38-year-old Lisa is the first straight female President of the United States, trying to rebuild the country after President Donald Trump's disastrous term, which apparently resulted in a new breed of healthy, insomniac super-criminals due to Trump approving of school breakfast programs and midnight basketball for inner-city children. In her attempts at doing so, Bart tries to upstage her by using Lisa's job as a foot in the door for a professional music career, which leads Lisa to be branded unpopular when Bart reveals to the public on live TV that Lisa will be imposing a tax to get the country out of debt (euphemized as a “temporary refund adjustment”). When Lisa meets with America's creditor nations who demand that America pay the countries back, Bart steps in and uses his skills at stalling debt collectors to save the day. In return, Lisa promises Bart to "legalize it."

Meanwhile, Homer has heard about gold buried by Abraham Lincoln, but Marge tells him that he is insane. When Homer finally locates the "gold," it is in fact a scroll that Lincoln had written on explaining that his "gold" is patriotic pride, but Homer, not understanding - or not esteeming - the metaphor as Marge did, angrily curses Lincoln for planting the idea in his head.

After the vision is over, Bart promises that he will change (even though he apparently learned nothing from it). Lisa finds Bart in the casino manager's office and tells him that the family's been kicked out after Homer pushed a waitress and Marge lost $20,000. Bart tells Lisa about his future vision where he has a rock band and a moped, while downplaying Lisa's future Presidency as "some government job."

[edit] Cultural references

  • The title comes from the movie Back to the Future.
  • This episode references Soylent Green.
  • Bart emerging from the case and Arthur Crandall proclaiming that Gabbo had become a real boy is a reference to Pinnochio.

[edit] Reception

In a 2003 article, "Bart to the Future" was listed as the worst Simpsons episode of all time by Entertainment Weekly, stating that, "while Bart to the Future was likely better than anything else on TV the week it first aired, even Mojo the monkey could've banged out a more inventive script," and that, "We didn't know it was physically possible for something to both suck AND blow."[1]
